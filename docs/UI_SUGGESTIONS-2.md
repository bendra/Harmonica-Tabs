The help is genuinely good — it's clear, concise, and written by someone who actually understands the app and its audience. A few specific things I like, and a few things I'd tweak:

**What works well**

The opening sentence is excellent. "HarpPilot helps you visualize playable scales and chords on a 10-hole diatonic harmonica, transpose tabs between keys, and play along while the app listens through your microphone." That's everything the app does in one sentence, no fluff. That's hard to write well and they've nailed it.

The **"Reading tabs" section** with the little reference table (4 = blow, -4 = draw, -4' = bend, etc.) is exactly right and in exactly the right place. New players coming from guitar tabs or sheet music need this immediately, and the table format makes it scannable. The note about overbends on holes 2, 3, and 8 being omitted is a nice detail — that's a real limitation of the diatonic that experienced players know but beginners would be confused by.

The **Tips section at the end** is genuinely useful and practical. "Major Pentatonic and Minor Pentatonic are great starting points for soloing — they have no bends in most positions" is exactly the advice you'd give someone sitting next to you. Same with the pitch detection tuning tips — they're specific and actionable rather than vague ("if it doesn't work, try adjusting settings").

The **"Choosing a harmonica for a song's key" section** is well handled. The ⇄ toggle is one of the cleverest things in the app and the help explains the logic clearly, including the smart detail that the Harmonica + Position list is "ordered practical-first: 1st, 2nd, 3rd, and 5th come at the top." That ordering is a considered design decision and the explanation justifies it nicely.

**What I'd improve**

The **"Scales workspace" and "Tabs workspace" sections feel slightly imbalanced in depth**. The Scales section gets a solid walkthrough, but the Tabs section is dense — Library, Transposer, Editor, and Tone Follow are all compressed into a short block with minimal breathing room between concepts. The Transposer explanation in particular ("Down / Up step the displayed tab one octave; Base resets to the saved first position") is accurate but assumes you already understand *why* you'd want to shift octaves. A sentence of context — like "useful when a melody sits too high or low for your comfortable range" — would help.

The **"Tone follow" paragraph** would benefit from its own named section header rather than being tucked at the end of the Tabs workspace bullet list. It's actually one of the most distinctive features of the app and it almost disappears in the current structure.

The **position system explanation** — "1st = harp's own key, 2nd = a fifth above (classic blues), and so on" — is correct but "and so on" is doing a lot of heavy lifting. Most beginners have no idea what 3rd position means or what key it gives you. Even just adding "(3rd = a tone below, classic minor)" would help. The app does compute this for you automatically, of course, but a new user reading the help page won't necessarily know why 3rd position exists or when to use it.

One minor **wording quirk**: the Transposer bullet says "Choose Tab clears the current source." That's technically accurate but the phrase "clears the current source" is a bit programmer-ish. Something like "Choose Tab lets you select a different saved tab to play back" would be more natural.

Overall though — it's tight, honest about what the app does and doesn't do (the fallback to simulated mic input is a nice touch of transparency), and doesn't waste the user's time. For a v0.5 app the help documentation is notably mature.
